Fortune 50 Company

Digital Merchandising Application

  • My Role: Product Designer
  • Duration: 10+ Months

Project Description

In 2017, the company sought to release their first vendor-facing item setup application for online retail. Coming from a completely manual process, this project was an opportunity to remove many user pain points while promoting positive business relationships between the company and their external vendor partners.

My Role

I was responsible for the user research and full design of the application. I produced all design deliverables and, ultimately, a prototype of the application built on Bootstrap. I also contributed additional artifacts such as training materials, user guides, and email copy. I worked closely with key stakeholders to understand their business process and pain points, and presented my ideas and prototype to stakeholders on different teams. I worked with a remote but in-house development team to realize the product vision.

Definitions

Digital Merchandising Team

The internal team that plans and manages which products are sold on the retail site. This includes the Merchandising Operations team (MerchOps), who is responsible for item setup.

Vendors

The companies who make the products that are sold on the retail site. Examples are Unilever, Proctor & Gamble, and Johnson & Johnson. I will refer to these individuals as vendors or vendor partners. This group is the end user of the product.

Digital Assets

Product data, such as images, descriptions, ingredients, and marketing copy. This data is used to setup the product for the retail site, and includes both customer facing data and data used for internal setup and ID generation. This is the information the MerchOps team needs from vendors to put products online.

The Challenge

Designing for a business process that was well established but entirely manual was a challenge to evaluate. Since there was nothing to “see”, it was necessary for me to collaborate closely with the merchandising team, carefully documenting their processes and digging into various leads until I could visualize their very complicated world and become an expert on the not-yet-existing product.

From there, it was my responsibility to fully design the front end of the application, including all the information architecture, various workflows, and interactions. Guiding stakeholders, who were not accustomed to working with a designer through the user experience design process, was an added challenge.

Discovery

While I was new to the merchandising world at the start of this project, the development team was not. Since everyone had ideas on what features to include, the fastest way to get everyone on the same page and learn about the project was to hold a few workshop sessions. I flew to where the development team is located and spent a week leading design workshops, ideating with the developers, and sketching out possible user flows.

Stakeholder Interviews

After the workshop sessions, I had enough context to start asking specific questions of the business stakeholders. I interviewed numerous members of the merchandising & MerchOps teams. I found that product data was being exchanged between MerchOps and vendors through a manual process of emails, spreadsheets, and FTPs. I documented the different workflows in the form of annotated flow charts.

Wireframes

I started translating the requirements I'd gathered into low fidelity sketches and wireframes using Axure. I iterated on different versions of the site map, trying to determine the best structure for the application. I frequently presented my work to the business and development teams to ensure I was fully capturing the requirements while also maintaining technical feasibility.

Prototype

I used Bootstrap to create my high fidelity designs. I choose to do this for a few different reasons:

  • It was the easiest way to apply the style guide in the Bootstrap theme
  • Coding meant minimal ambiguity in the visual design for the dev team
  • The prototype was ultimately used for usability testing

Usability Testing

I worked with the merchandising team to invite a handful of select vendors on-site to test the prototype. We had a total of 18 participants across 10 vendor companies. Each of the participants tested the prototype in our usability lab with a member of our user research team. Because of the complex nature of the merchandising world and the business implications surrounding the application, I was present during testing to take notes and answer any non-usability related questions.

Final Design

Usability feedback was incorporated into the designs. In addition to the prototype, static comps were created on an as-needed basis to describe business rules and interactions. I worked closely with the developers to make sure the documentation was sufficient enough to do their work.

I also worked with the business and product to create:

  • User Guide
  • FAQs
  • Training Decks
  • Automated Emails - when to send them, who to send them to, copy, attachments, etc

Next Steps

The application launches late summer of 2017. After the initial launch, we plan to iterate the designs based on user feedback and to explore implementation of features requested during usability testing, such as:

  • Functionality for vendors to manage the full range of their products sold on our retail site
  • Further build out the internal facing application to support business vendor partnerships
  • Dashboards and reports for managing product sales and popularity
  • UI for merchandising team to set image requirements at the product type level
